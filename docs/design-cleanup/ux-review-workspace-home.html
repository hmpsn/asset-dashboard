UX Review · single-workspace home

Workspace Home
has nine front doors.

A heuristic review of WorkspaceHome — the screen an operator lives in once they pick a workspace. It's the richest surface in the product and the most data-complete. That's also the problem: it presents up to nine equal hero metrics, four representations of one health score, and setup nudges in three places, then buries the actual worklist below all of it. This is the sharper sibling of the Command Center's hierarchy issue. Every fix reuses primitives you ship.

What's already right

✓

Data freshness is honest. A relative "updated 4m ago", a "Stale" flag past an hour, and a manual Refresh — operators always know how current the numbers are.

✓

StatCards deep-link. Every metric routes to its detail surface (audit, analytics, ranks). The cards are navigation, not just decoration.

✓

Onboarding is dismissible and remembered. The checklist persists its dismissed state per workspace — it won't nag after setup.

✓

Graceful empty metrics. Each StatCard has a real "—" + "Connect GSC/GA4" fallback rather than a broken zero.

F3

Purple on the Users metric breaks the admin-only law

Medium · color law

The Users StatCard sets iconColor="#a78bfa" — purple. Your fourth law is explicit: purple is for admin AI only and never appears in a client-facing data view. Using it on a core traffic metric both violates the rule and erodes the one signal that's supposed to mean "this is AI/admin." Several sibling cards also hardcode hex (#22d3ee, #4ade80, #f87171) instead of the tokens.

02

The pattern across both screens

The Command Center and Workspace Home share one root cause: the product surfaces everything it knows at equal weight. The data layer is excellent, so every signal earns a card — but design's job is to rank them. Three system-level moves fix most of it:

1

Cap heroes at three. A "hero metric" budget per screen forces a choice about what leads. Everything else is a secondary tier.

2

One concept, one widget. Health score, setup progress, and the worklist should each have exactly one representation — extract shared components (NeedsAttention, a single health block) into the system so screens can't diverge.

3

Color means status, never chrome or plain data. Mint = action, blue = data, emerald = success, amber/red = warn/danger, purple = admin AI only. A repo sweep enforces it cheaply.
