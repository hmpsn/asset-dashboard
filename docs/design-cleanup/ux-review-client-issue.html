The Issue —
already the best room in the house.

A review of TheIssueClientPage (verdict-first spine branch) — the evergreen client money surface. This screen has clearly already had a serious design pass: it leads with a dollar verdict, kills purple, uses honest provenance language, and pushes detail behind progressive disclosure. So this isn't a rescue — it's polish. The residual issues are about how many proof surfaces stack before the client can act, and a few consistency seams that the design system could close for good.

The findings

F1

The plan — the one place they act — sits below three proof surfaces

Medium · hierarchy

The flag-OFF branch deliberately leads with the plan ("plan LEADS, proof FOLLOWS," per the file's own comment). The spine branch inverts that: verdict → outcome count → money frame → loop → then the Content Plan at slot 5. For a check-signer the dollar lead is right — but "Request this" (the only retainer action on the page) now sits beneath three stacked proof bands. A client who's convinced by the verdict may never scroll to the action.

RecommendationKeep the verdict as the lead (it's the hook), then bring the Content Plan up directly under it so conviction flows straight into action. Let the deeper proof (outcome count, money frame, wins) follow. Worth an A/B: "verdict → plan → proof" vs the current "verdict → proof → plan" on request-rate.

F2

The ROI dashboard renders twice on one page

Medium · redundancy

Slot 3 mounts <ROIDashboard compact /> and the "Under the hood" disclosure mounts <ROIDashboard compact={false} /> again. Two instances of the same dollar story on one screen — the client can open "Under the hood" and see the ROI numbers a second time, with no signal that it's the same data expanded. Risk of "wait, which number is right?" plus a second data fetch.

RecommendationRender ROI once. Either keep the compact money frame at slot 3 and let "Under the hood" hold only the methodology/tables (not a full second dashboard), or make the compact frame itself expandable in place. One dollar story, one mount.

F3

"Your turn" and "Ask your strategist" are two pending-work surfaces at opposite ends

Low · redundancy

Slot 0 is the ActionQueueStrip ("Your turn" — pending decisions). Slot 4 is the IssueLoopFooter ("What needs me — the full pending-decisions queue / loop"). Both represent "things awaiting you," bookending the page. A client could act in the top strip, then meet a second, differently-shaped queue lower down and wonder if it's new work.

RecommendationMake the relationship explicit: the top strip is the count/jump ("3 decisions waiting ↓"), the footer is the detail. Same visual language, and ideally the strip anchors down to the loop rather than being a parallel list.

F4

Section headings come in three different forms

Low · consistency

Across the page, a "section header" is sometimes a muted <h2 class="t-label"> uppercase mini-label (Money, What's working), sometimes a SectionCard title bar, and sometimes a <summary> row (Under the hood). Three weights doing one job means the page's outline is hard to skim — the eye can't tell a section start from a card header.

RecommendationDefine one section-header treatment in the design system (the t-label kicker is the right call here) and use it for every top-level section; reserve SectionCard headers for cards within a section. Consistent rhythm makes a long scroll legible.

F5

The tinted-gradient card is reinvented per section instead of being a StatCard variant

Low · system drift

The verdict shell hand-rolls from-teal-500/8 … border-teal-500/15; the outcome StatCards pass className="bg-gradient-to-br from-emerald-500/10 … border-emerald-500/20". The "tinted gradient + matching border" treatment is lovely — and it's being re-typed with slightly different opacities each place it appears. That's exactly the drift a design system exists to prevent.

RecommendationPromote it to a first-class StatCard / card tone prop (tone="emerald" → the gradient + border baked in at one canonical opacity). Then the verdict hero and the outcome band share one definition, and future surfaces get it for free.

02

Verdict on the verdict screen

This screen is the proof that the team can execute the design language at a high level — it should be the reference the other surfaces are pulled toward. Its findings are nearly all polish and system-consolidation, not fixes: bring the action up under the verdict (F1), dedupe ROI (F2), and lift the recurring patterns (gradient card, section header, attention row) into shared components so the rest of the app inherits this quality instead of re-deriving it.
